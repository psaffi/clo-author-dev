% Securities Lending and Portfolio Decisions

We begin our analysis by examining whether mutual funds adjust their portfolio positions in response to securities lending activity. Our baseline specification regresses monthly position changes on lagged lending indicators, controlling for unobserved heterogeneity through a saturated set of fixed effects.

\subsection{Baseline Results}

Table~\ref{table_baseline} presents the baseline results. The dependent variable is the percentage change in the number of shares of stock $i$ held by fund $j$ in month $t$ ($PosChange_{i,j,t}$). Our key explanatory variable is $OnLoan_{i,j,t-1}$, which measures whether a fund's position in stock $i$ was on loan in the previous month.

In column~(1), we estimate the relationship for the full sample of funds, including security$\times$time, fund$\times$time, and fund$\times$security fixed effects, with standard errors double-clustered by fund and time. The coefficient on $OnLoan_{i,j,t-1}$ is $-0.2069$ and statistically significant at the 1\% level ($t = -4.23$). Funds reduce their positions in stocks that they lend.

\subsection{Active vs.\ Passive Funds}

A key prediction of the information acquisition hypothesis is that only active fund managers---who can freely adjust their portfolios---should respond to the lending signal. Passive fund managers, constrained to track their benchmark index, cannot trade on the information even if they observe it.

Column~(2) of Table~\ref{table_baseline} tests this prediction by interacting $OnLoan_{i,j,t-1}$ with an indicator for actively managed funds. The interaction term $Active_j \times OnLoan_{i,j,t-1}$ is $-0.3382$ and highly significant ($t = -5.33$), while the uninteracted $OnLoan_{i,j,t-1}$ coefficient becomes small and statistically insignificant ($0.0133$, $t = 0.80$). The position reduction is entirely driven by active funds.

Columns~(3)--(5) restrict the sample to active funds only. Column~(3) confirms the baseline effect with a coefficient of $-0.3271$ ($t = -5.39$). Column~(4) replaces the continuous lending measure with the extensive margin indicator $D(Lender)_{i,j,t-1}$, which captures whether a fund is a lender. The coefficient of $-1.9165$ ($t = -4.18$) indicates that active lending funds reduce positions significantly more than non-lending active funds. Column~(5) includes both measures simultaneously; the continuous $OnLoan$ measure dominates ($-0.3206$, $t = -5.49$), while the extensive margin indicator becomes insignificant, suggesting that the intensity of lending---not merely lending participation---drives the trading response.
