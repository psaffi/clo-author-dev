% Introduction — Securities Lending and Information Acquisition

When a mutual fund lends shares from its portfolio to a short seller, does the fund learn something valuable?  Short sellers are among the best-informed participants in financial markets, and a large body of evidence shows that their trades predict negative future returns.\footnote{See, among others, \cite{diamond_verrecchia:87}, \cite{boehmer_jones_zhang:08}, \cite{engelberg_reed_ringgenberg:2012}, and \cite{karpoff_lou:10}.}  By participating in the securities lending market, fund managers observe, in real time and at the security level, which of their holdings are being borrowed.  This information is private: aggregate short interest is published only with a delay, and position-level lending data are not publicly available.  We ask whether active fund managers exploit this private information channel by adjusting their portfolios in response to lending activity, and, if so, how to identify the mechanism through which information is acquired and acted upon.

We study these questions using a unique regulatory dataset from the Deutsche Bundesbank that records the security-level lending positions of all German open-end equity mutual funds.  Our sample covers 320 funds from 36 fund families over December 2014 to December 2018.  Crucially, this period coincides with the European Union's mandatory short position disclosure regime, which requires public disclosure of net short positions exceeding 0.5\% of a company's issued share capital (\citealp{jank_smajlbegovic:2015, jank_roling_smajlbegovic2020}).  This regime provides an institutional source of identification that has no direct analog in existing U.S.\ studies: we can test whether the private information embedded in lending activity predicts fund trading specifically before a large short position is publicly revealed, establishing a clean timing test of the information acquisition mechanism.

Our central contribution is to identify the information channel through which lending activity affects portfolio decisions.  The key source of identification is the interaction between a fund's private lending signal and the subsequent mandatory disclosure of a large short position.  If active lenders are extracting information from the pattern of borrowing demand, the position reduction they undertake should be amplified precisely when that demand precedes a public confirmation event---a new disclosure that the market can observe.  We find exactly this pattern.

Our main empirical findings are as follows.

\emph{First}, active funds reduce their positions in stocks they lend.  A one-standard-deviation increase in lending activity is associated with a 0.33 percentage point reduction in the monthly position growth rate, and this effect is absent for passive funds, who cannot freely adjust holdings (Table~\ref{table_baseline}).  The active-passive split is the primary placebo test: passive funds hold the same stocks and participate in the same lending market, but their portfolios are mechanically constrained.  The fact that only active funds respond rules out mechanical explanations such as share recalls for proxy voting or collateral-driven rebalancing, which would affect both fund types equally.

\emph{Second}, we confirm that lending conveys information using mandatory disclosure events as an external validation.  Within the disclosure sample---stocks that eventually receive a public large short-position disclosure---the baseline position reduction for active lenders is $-0.28$ percentage points per month (Table~\ref{table_disclosure}, column~1).  This effect grows to $-0.80$ to $-0.86$ percentage points when the lending activity in month $t$ precedes a new disclosure event in month $t+1$, nearly tripling the magnitude.  Critically, the amplification is \emph{asymmetric in time}: only the lead disclosure indicator amplifies the effect; contemporaneous and lagged disclosure interactions are statistically indistinguishable from zero (Table~\ref{table_timing}).  Passive funds show no response at any horizon (Table~\ref{table_disclosure}, column~8).  This temporal pattern---pre-announcement reductions by lenders, no post-announcement adjustment---is inconsistent with a mechanical story and is consistent only with active information acquisition through the lending process.

\emph{Third}, information flows are strongest within fund families, with the same portfolio manager's other lending funds generating the largest incremental reduction (Table~\ref{table_family}).  The effect attenuates with organizational distance: lending by other managers within the same family generates a smaller but still detectable response, consistent with centralized lending desks serving as conduits for within-family information sharing (\citealp{pool2012no}).  We use passive fund lending in the same family as an alternative, plausibly more exogenous measure of lending demand (Table~\ref{table_passive_iv}), and the results are robust; a formal IV design with a reported first stage is deferred to the revision.

\emph{Fourth}, the informativeness of the lending signal is amplified in concentrated markets (Table~\ref{table_market_structure}).  When fewer agents intermediate lending for a given stock---measured by the number of active lending agents, the family's HHI of lending concentration, or the inverse of the number of stocks lent---each lending contract carries more signal about the identity and size of the short position being built.  These heterogeneity results sharpen the economic interpretation: information acquisition is not uniform but varies predictably with market structure.

These findings speak to a central tension in the securities lending literature.  \cite{EvansFerreiraPrado2017} document that active funds that participate in lending underperform otherwise similar non-lending funds by 0.5\%--0.7\% per year, a puzzle they attribute to fund family incentives that prevent managers from acting on short-selling demand signals.  Our results are consistent with managers responding to the lending signal when they have portfolio discretion, and the EU disclosure design allows us to confirm that the mechanism is information rather than mechanics.  Whether this trading response is sufficient to overcome the overall cost of lending participation---and thereby resolve the EFP underperformance puzzle---requires a direct performance comparison that we defer to the revision; the underperformance documented by \cite{EvansFerreiraPrado2017} may reflect institutional constraints, lending revenue drag, or both.

Our paper is most closely related to \cite{Honkanen2025}, who uses hand-collected U.S.\ SEC filings on the ten largest fund families to study securities lending and trading at the quarterly frequency.  \cite{Honkanen2025} establishes that active funds reduce portfolio weights in lent stocks and that information spills over within fund families from passive lenders to active traders.  We extend this work in three ways that jointly constitute our main contribution.  First, our Bundesbank data cover the \emph{universe} of German funds rather than the ten largest U.S.\ families, eliminating sample-selection concerns.  Second, we observe lending at the monthly rather than quarterly frequency.  Third---and most importantly---the EU mandatory short position disclosure regime provides an independent, institutional identification of the information channel.  The disclosure interaction test cannot be conducted in the U.S.\ setting because U.S.\ short position data are not reported at the position level with the same detail and timeliness.  Without an external validation of this kind, it is difficult to rule out alternative explanations for the lending-trading link; our paper provides it.

\cite{Nurisso2026} develops a theoretical framework in which a lender learns about a stock's value by observing whether a short seller borrows shares, and shows that active lenders must raise fees as a commitment device to avoid recalling lent shares.  Our empirical finding that active funds trade on lending information while passive funds do not is consistent with short sellers preferring to borrow from lenders who cannot exploit the information (\citealp{PaliaSokolinski2024}).  \cite{ChagueGiovannettiHerskovic2025} identify a distinct but related channel in the Brazilian market: brokers intermediating securities lending transactions leak information about skilled short sellers' directional bets to their other clients.  Our paper demonstrates that the lender \emph{itself} acquires and acts on the information, even without broker-mediated leakage.

We also contribute to the literature on short-sale constraints and price efficiency (\citealp{saffi_sigurdsson:08, prado_saffi_sturgess:14}), information production in financial markets (\citealp{Goldstein_yang:2017, grossman_stiglitz:80}), and short position disclosure (\citealp{jank_roling_smajlbegovic2020, jones_reed_waller:16}).  \cite{prado_2015} shows that institutions buy shares in response to increases in lending fees; our paper documents the complementary short-side signal: when borrowing demand is high, active managers reduce their exposure to avoid capital losses.  \cite{cohen_diether_malloy:07} establish that demand-side shifts in the borrowing market---rather than supply-side constraints---predict negative returns, directly motivating why the private demand signal observed through lending conveys valuable information.
