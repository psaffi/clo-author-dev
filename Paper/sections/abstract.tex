We show that mutual fund managers exploit the securities lending market as an information acquisition channel. Using regulatory data from the Deutsche Bundesbank that records security-level lending positions for the universe of German open-end equity mutual funds, we find that active funds reduce their holdings in stocks they lend by 0.33 percentage points per month. The effect is absent for passive funds. To identify the information acquisition mechanism, we interact lending with mandatory short position disclosure events under EU regulation. The trading response to lending more than doubles when a disclosure event follows in the subsequent month, with interaction coefficients of $-0.80$ to $-0.86$ percentage points. Only the lead disclosure amplifies the effect; contemporaneous and lagged disclosures are uninformative. Information flows most strongly through same-manager fund lending within families and is amplified in concentrated lending markets with fewer active agents. Our findings demonstrate that the securities lending market serves as a conduit for private information about short-selling demand, enabling active fund managers to make informed portfolio decisions.
